Rock Paper Scissors 2.0 was game born out of the following twitter thread. The game adds an interesting twist to the classical game and in this post I will discuss the game, its formulation as an MDP, the game theoretical solution, and an analytic dive into the best possible play.

Formulating the Game

The game itself isn't too difficult to formulate. We start off with three coins in between two players. Each of these coins is the "rock" coin, the "paper" coin, and the "scissors" coin. The two players then play a normal game of rock paper scissors. If one of the two players won, they slide the coin which represents the move they just played one space towards themself. The players continue playing RPS and sliding coins until one of the players wins with the coin which is already on their side of the board. This player then wins the entire game. So, suppose on the very first turn you (being at the bottom) threw paper and your opponent (being at the top) threw scissors. Then, we know that scissors beats paper and he moves the scissors coin closer to himself.

It is now clear that it is a much riskier move for you to throw paper again, because then your opponent can automatically win the game by throwing scissors and claiming the scissors coin. On the other hand, your opponent knows this and if they knew that you would never throw paper then they can always throw rock knowing that neither your scissors nor your rock would be able to beat them. This conundrum is at the heart of what makes this game interesting. Moreover, it begs the question what is the optimal way to play in a game where we need to balance the risk of losing with the probability of winning. This article attempts to go into a good amount of detail in finding a sufficient answer to this question.

Setting up the Game Theory for Optimal Play

Let us start by investigating the ideas for the simpler game of normal rock-paper-scissors. We will be talking about a "mixed strategy Nash equillibirum." This refers to the situation where each player is allowed to use a "mixed strategy" which means that they can randomly decide between all of their potential options. A "Nash equillibirum" refers to a game theory concept describing a situation where two players, both knowing their opponent's strategy, will still opt for the same strategy they have previously chosen, because any change to their current strategy would actually leave them worse off (assuming their opponent also keeps the same strategy.) When both players feel this way, the strategies are at a Nash equillibirum. When each of those strategies are allowed to be random choices as we just described, then altogether we arrive at a "mixed strategy Nash equillibirum." So, in the context of RPS, we are looking for each player's probability of throwing rock, paper, or scissors if they are able to know their opponent's strategy beforehand. If you have thought about this problem before it becomes pretty obvious that the best strategy is to throw each with probability $1/3$ because for instance if you threw rock with probability 1/2 then your opponent could just choose to throw paper always and they would instantly have a 50% chance or greater of beating you.
